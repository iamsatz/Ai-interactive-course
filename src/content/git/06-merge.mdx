<ConceptSection id="merge" term="Merge" category="git" oneLiner="Joins two branches. Full history preserved — every commit.">

When you **merge** a branch into `main`, every commit from that branch becomes part of `main`'s history. The branch's full timeline — every intermediate commit, every fix, every "oops" commit — is preserved.

This is useful when you want complete auditability: every step in the development process is traceable.

<Diagram />

For AI-assisted work, standard merge has a downside: AI sessions tend to generate many small commits with messages like "fix typo," "try this approach," "undo last change." Merging them all into `main` creates a noisy, hard-to-read history.

This is why **squash & merge** is usually preferred for AI work — but understanding standard merge helps you choose deliberately.

<Analogy>Merging two Figma files with every layer history intact. Complete, but potentially messy. Every intermediate state is preserved. Useful when you need full traceability; less useful when you just want a clean milestone.</Analogy>

<WhyItMatters accent="var(--accent-git)">Use merge when full commit history matters — for regulatory traceability, collaborative codebases, or when every step in the process needs to be auditable. For typical AI-assisted sessions, squash & merge produces a much cleaner result.</WhyItMatters>
