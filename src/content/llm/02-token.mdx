<ConceptSection id="token" term="Token" category="llm" oneLiner="The chunk the model reads. Roughly a word or syllable.">

The model does not read letter by letter, or word by word. It reads in **tokens** — chunks of text that are roughly syllables or common word fragments. The word "strawberry" becomes two tokens: "Straw" and "berry." A space before a word is often its own token. Numbers and punctuation are tokenized differently from prose.

This is how the model sees all text — including your prompt and its own response. Neither side ever sees raw letters.

<Diagram />

Tokenization matters for two practical reasons: **cost** and **limits**. Most AI APIs price by token. And every model has a finite context window measured in tokens — not words or pages.

<Analogy>"Strawberry" is two tokens. The model never sees letters — only chunks. It's reading in syllable-sized bites, not character by character. Understanding this helps you predict cost and explains why certain words count as "more" than others.</Analogy>

<WhyItMatters accent="var(--accent-llm)">Tokens determine cost and context limits. Every word you type — and every word the model returns — costs tokens. Long documents, long conversations, long outputs: all eat from the same budget.</WhyItMatters>
