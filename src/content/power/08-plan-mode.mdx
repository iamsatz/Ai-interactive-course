<ConceptSection id="plan-mode" term="Plan Mode" category="power" oneLiner="Claude writes a plan before doing anything. You approve, edit, or reject it first.">

Plan mode is an explicit "stop and think" step. Instead of jumping straight into edits, Claude proposes a structured plan: what files it will touch, what changes it will make, what order, what to test. You read it. You approve, edit, or reject.

This is the opposite of how chat normally works. Normally you ask, the model acts. In plan mode, you ask, the model plans, you confirm, then it acts.

<Diagram />

When plan mode earns its keep:

- **Multi-file refactors.** Seeing the full file list before any edit happens.
- **Anything you'd need to undo.** Reading the plan is cheaper than reading the diff later.
- **Unfamiliar codebases.** The plan forces Claude to articulate its mental model — and you can catch wrong assumptions.
- **Scoping a new feature.** Use the plan itself as your design doc.

Plan mode adds latency (one extra round trip for the plan). It also adds clarity. For trivial tasks, skip it. For anything non-trivial, demand it.

<Analogy>Asking a designer to sketch the wireframe before they touch Figma. You can change direction on a sketch in seconds. Changing direction once a high-fidelity comp is built costs an afternoon.</Analogy>

<WhyItMatters accent="var(--accent-power)">Plan mode is how you reclaim control of an agent loop. Without it, Claude proposes-and-acts in a single step. With it, you get a checkpoint — a moment where wrong assumptions are surfaced before they become wrong code.</WhyItMatters>
